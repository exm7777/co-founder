# Offer heuristics: distilled from operators who run this at scale

Field-tested mechanics, each with its trap. These are the lens the offer skill applies to the
founder's specifics; none of them outranks the founder's own numbers from the vault. The
examples and figures in this file are heuristics, never receipts: research still supplies the
external comparable for any recommendation that ships.

## Designing the offer and its ladder

- **Paid is a different product, never "more of the free thing."** The paid layer sells
  transformation, access, accountability, proprietary artifacts, applied depth. The free
  layer proves taste and evidence standards. Trap: hiding ordinary content behind a paywall
  trains buyers to price-compare it against cheap subscriptions instead of valuing the
  outcome.
- **The free tier ends right after the aha.** Free must let the right person feel the value,
  then stop before it substitutes for paid. Gate what scales utility: speed, access,
  automation, templates, feedback, implementation depth. Trap: "more free value" grows the
  audience while shrinking the business when the full utility loop is free.
- **Every lower tier gets the substitution test before it exists.** Name the paid behavior
  it should create, the feature that stays visibly gated, and the metric that would prove
  cannibalization within one launch cycle. Trap: framing it as "free tier: yes or no"
  instead of "does this tier route people toward paid or quietly absorb them."
- **The cautionary case:** one operator added a $24/mo basic tier beneath a $70/mo standard.
  Many signups, few upgrades, fewer standard-tier joins, churn doubled, workload up. Cheap
  substitution plus operating drag. Trap: reading signups as validation while revenue
  quality, retention, and positioning all degrade.

## Pricing it

- **A cap must map to a real operating constraint:** onboarding attention, cohort size,
  support capacity. Buyers trust scarcity they can understand ("more members would make it
  worse"). One operator's 200-member cap filled fast but proved wrong for the experience; a
  later 50-spot cohort worked because the constraint was real. Trap: picking 100 because it
  sounds marketable leaves no defense when someone asks why the offer must close.
- **Grandfathering makes price steps fair.** Every price-increase message says plainly that
  existing members keep their rate while active. It belongs in the price block, the FAQ, the
  checkout copy, not buried as a retention detail. Trap: without the lock-in promise,
  urgency reads as extraction.
- **The price-step close is factual clauses:** "$X/month. N spots at this price. When
  they're gone: $Y. Members keep their rate." Each sentence verifiable or operationally
  honored, answering the buyer's questions in order: price now, how many, what changes, am
  I protected. Trap: emotional garnish ("almost gone", "last chance") adds claims that must
  be true at publication and stay true, or they are liabilities.
- **New-member deadlines and existing-member repricing are different moves.** Prospects
  choosing to join before a public price change: a short real deadline works. Repricing
  current members is an account-management event with notice and churn risk. Trap: importing
  launch hype into billing changes punishes loyalty.

## Closing it

- **Anchor value before naming the price.** Total the honest reference frame first (what it
  replaces: tools, courses, coaching, operating costs), then reveal the lower price as a
  resolved calculation. Trap: fake math. An anchor that cannot be defended in plain language
  gets cut, not inflated.
- **The close is a fixed sequence, not a pile of parts:** the program enters, pieces get
  named, proof validates the pieces, the price loop opens with the anchor, price drops,
  the bigger commitment lifts order value, then guarantee, bonuses, future pace, urgency,
  final call. Each beat answers the next objection before the buyer forms it. Trap: the
  same sequence with fake urgency or filler bonuses is a trust leak wearing a structure.
- **Guarantee length follows the first credible result, never a copied number.** Match the
  window to how long the buyer needs to experience real value, the refund risk the business
  carries, and the category's anxiety. Trap: shortest-because-safer and
  longest-because-persuasive are both wrong when they ignore the buyer's actual path.
- **Annual versus monthly is a tension to test, not a religion.** Cash-per-buyer bottleneck
  plus established trust: lead annual. Real urgency attached to a monthly deadline, colder
  audience, first conversion mattering most: lead monthly, frame annual as the upgrade.
  Trap: forcing order value at the expense of conversion, or vice versa, without testing.
- **Urgency is legal only when the constraint is real enough to name:** a dated price
  increase or a true capacity limit, stated with what changes, when, and why, reflected on
  checkout and follow-up too. Trap: "spots are filling fast" with no operational meaning.
  If the claim cannot be enforced consistently, it does not appear.

## Launching it

- **Launch when the audience asks paid questions.** Requests for accountability, access,
  faster implementation, networking: those fund. Requests for more free content: not yet;
  collect paid-intent signals first (waitlist questions that name the price, calls,
  replies). Trap: launching from vague interest; a waitlist fills with free-seekers when
  payment was never made explicit.
- **High-ticket launches into the warm slice, never cold traffic.** Existing engaged
  subscribers outbuy cold registrants by multiples; find the engaged slice (clicks, replies,
  attendance, survey answers) before asking for a real monthly commitment. Trap: treating a
  big list as a buyer list; cold subscribers inflate expectations, then convert weakly and
  churn.
